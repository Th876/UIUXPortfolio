Cake Wrecks

Baking a Sweeter Experience

Context: Collaborative project at Arizona State University

Challenge

Cake Wrecks is the go-to blog for fans seeking a good laugh at baking mishaps. However, its outdated design and tricky navigation make it difficult for fans to fully enjoy the content.

So, the question was:
How might we make Cake Wrecks' interface not just easier, but also more fun to navigate?

Our Solution

We started by collaboratively identifying key usability issues and gathering user feedback. From these insights, I took on the task of redesigning the website from the ground up.

The goal? A modern interface with enhanced navigation that allows users to seamlessly explore, share, send, and inquire about cake fails across all devices.

Initial Research

Understanding the User

To understand user frustration, we conducted quantitative and qualitative research, like heuristic evaluations, surveys, A/B and observation tests and shared our findings with each other.

This deep dive on the old website guided us in our individual tasks, including redesigning the website's user interface.

Since we wanted a clearer picture of our target audience, we kick-started our user research with a screener survey. This survey was our first step in filtering potential users who engaged with cake fails and baking communities.

Observation Test: Round One

With insights from our 20 screener survey respondents, we selected 6 participants to move on to the next step—our moderated observation test on the old Cake Wrecks website. This wasn’t just any test; it featured real-time scenarios and tasks that uncovered how users navigated the site, where they stumbled, and what made them smile (or frown).

Before jumping into the tasks, participants completed a brief pre-test questionnaire to capture their initial expectations. After they completed the observation test, they did a post-test questionnaire to express their final thoughts.

Observation Test: Round Two

The second observation test I conducted, involving the same six participants from the first round, revealed that while users loved the website's overall redesign, opinions on the new interactive map feature were divided.

Participants’ Feedback on the Interactive Map

Positive 👍🏼

  • “The new map is fantastic! I love how it shows all the bakeries at once in such an interactive way. It’s so much easier to find what I’m looking for.”
  • “The map really adds a fun element to finding bakeries. Loving this new feature.”
  • “The map's a feature I didn’t know I needed! Searching doesn't feel so tedious anymore.”

Mixed 😐

  • “The map is cool, but I still prefer a good old list. If the old design’s list was just a bit cleaner and more organized, it would be perfect.”
  • “I like the map for browsing, but for quick access, a simpler list would be more my speed—especially if it were less cluttered.”
  • “The map is appealing, but sometimes I just want a simple list. If the list view were more organized, I’d probably use that instead.”

Some users enjoyed how the map displays bakeries in a dynamic card format, while others preferred the simplicity of a straightforward list. This mix of reactions showed that the map’s pop-up feature might not be the best fit for everyone.

Given this feedback, it’s clear there’s still room for improvement in how I present the bakery information.

ViewSecond Observation Test w/ Post-Test Questionnaire

Refining the Design

To tackle the mixed reviews, I went back to the drawing board. This iterative design process led me to design a more organized list that makes finding bakeries both intuitive and efficient. From there, I began the A/B testing.

I brought in six new participants from the screener survey pool who had an interest in cake fails. They performed the task of finding a bakery in Atlanta, Georgia, and shared their thoughts on the design.

Interestingly, the test results showed that users were equally split between the interactive map and the list, which led to my final redesign decision—a toggle feature. This feature allows users to toggle between the interactive map view (now an embedded map) and a list view.

Here's how each option of the toggle feature serves different user needs:

*Overlay extends to full screen in the actual prototype
  • Interactive Map View: A modern and engaging way for users to discover bakeries, enhancing the browsing experience. This is the default view to address user frustration with outdated features.
  • List View: A structured and straightforward method to find specific bakeries. It is an alternative for users who prefer a more traditional, organized approach to navigation.

Takeaways

Impact

The Cake Wrecks redesign has transformed the way users interact with the site. After implementing the new features and putting them to the test, users felt the site was not only easier to navigate but also way more engaging.

The final design not only enhances the user experience by making the site more inclusive and efficient, but it also sets the stage for future growth and continued user satisfaction—it’s a win-win for everyone!

What I learned

This project has been a valuable learning experience. Here are some key things I learned throughout the process:

  • The Power of Iterative Design:By running multiple observation and A/B tests, I saw firsthand how refining designs based on user feedback boosts usability and effectiveness.
  • Interpreting A/B Test Results:A/B testing sometimes reveals complex user preferences. Combining elements from different versions may often be more effective than choosing just one.
  • Importance of Accessibility: Accessibility goes beyond compliance. Adding a floating accessibility icon with a hide option demonstrates the need for equity-focused design since all users should have a fair and equitable experience.
  • Designing for Simplicity:This redesign reinforced that simplicity in design is key. Finding the balance between functionality and ease of use makes for a more enjoyable user experience.
